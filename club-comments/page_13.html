Kuiper | 06-03-14, 1:16 AM
Saya no Uta may be one of the most (personally) impactful pieces of media I have ever consumed.

For me, the most interesting part of the story was


Fuminori's slow transformation into a complete (figurative) monster. At the beginning of the story, Fuminori can obviously be distinguished as a human protagonist, and while he certainly isn't affable, his actions and the motivations behind them are easy to understand, and even feel sympathy for. When he emotionally crushes Yoh by giving her a heartless rejection, his actions certainly aren't right, but it's hard to condemn him for them. After all, he is simply trying to avoid what he perceives to be a disgusting monster, and treating her like dirt is the only way he can get his ex-friends out of his life. He does his utmost to make it brief, and the abuse is entirely non-physical. He even acknowledges in the back of his mind (and expresses a twinge of regret) that the creature he is now chasing out of his life was once a friend. He's no hero, but it's hard to think of him as a bad man.

From that point on, you spend several hours both in and out of his head. And somewhere, toward the end, we are struck with a crowning moment of realization. When Fuminori lures Koji to a secluded area and pushes him into the well. It is the perfect pre-meditated murder, an act that he both planned and committed with pure glee. And then comes the realization: between the beginning of the story and where we have now arrived, somewhere, somehow, something has gone terribly and horribly wrong.

I think one of the things that makes the transformation most interesting is that, like his rejection of Yoh, most of Fuminori's actions are committed and narrated in a way that makes the reader sympathetic to them. Even the act of cannibalism, while certainly repulsive on a physical level, isn't something that one can easily condemn him for; his descent into the practice is so slow and innocent that it's hard to realize where things go wrong. I think that's one of the things that struck me most about Saya no Uta: it managed to make Fuminori's adoption of the practice of cannibalism seem innocent. The narration required to do this is beautiful beyond words.

I think the most horrifying image that Saya no Uta presented me with was this: http://imgur.com/UvHwCX9

Saya no Uta is a visual novel full of graphic gory images depicting the flesh-covered walls, dismembered body parts in Fuminori's house, and the abominations that inhabit Fuminori's world. And yet, after reading the VN, I can't pick out a more horrifying image than the one above.

This CG is used in a very special moment. It's used to present the reader with exactly the image that Fuminori sees in the story's final acts. He sees Saya's "human" form, with full knowledge of the unspeakable horrors that she has subjected Yoh to, a fate far, far worse than death. He gazes into her eyes and see the hatred and malice that burns within them, and her cruel sadistic pleasure in knowing Yoh's suffering. In fact, he is gazing into the face of what can only be described as a pure monster in all but (ironically) the literal sense. And, as he gazes into that face, he likes what he sees.

The effect is absolutely chilling, far more horrifying than any of the gory, ugly, disgusting, and otherwise frightening images that the VN presents.

A friend of mine put it like this:

That fucking image. It is that portrait of Saya and everything that builds up to it that makes you swear at the game's developers out loud. The pinnacle of the Lovecraftian horror effect. It is the simple, brutal knowledge of what Saya really is and what she has done, in gruesome stark contrast to the face and the music, two things that you originally welcomed with open arms when you first encountered them. So in a sense, it is the extreme feeling of betrayal. The strongest betrayal of the player's emotions in any work of fiction I have ever heard of and which likely will not be surpassed in my lifetime.

For the rest of the game, I felt a growing thirst for a kind of primitive, bloodthirsty vengeance. But it was really something much more than that. And then at one point, the game itself explained my pitch black emotions to me. Saya needed to be destroyed, but not for the reasons I had in mind. Not for revenge, not for justice, but simply because she is so disturbing, so WRONG, that the only acceptable outcome that could ever hope to put my mind at ease is to witness her complete and utter extermination.
