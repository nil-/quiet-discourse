Personally, I rate things for my own benefit. Keeping ratings as a scale of basically "how much did I enjoy myself watching this" makes it a useful, interesting way to keep tabs on how my tastes change over time and what kinds of things I find interesting in anime. I can make more accurate statements about how I feel about certain things, for example when people say "this season wasn't very good in comparison to previous seasons of anime I have watched" and with my scores I can pretty accurately say whether or not I really do feel this way or if a few good/bad shows are heavily influencing how I am interpreting a much larger set of anime. I also prescribe to the thought that my scores can be a decent quick-glance indicator of how I feel about shows, and any following discussion that is tainted by those scores being the focus isn't the fault of the score existing but what the other person chooses to do with it. If the exact score is the real focus of the conversation, we were never really talking about the anime anyway.

Veronin | 04-17-14, 2:50 PM
Scores are useful for me since it eliminates the "Hey, what did you think of X?" and "How did you think X compared to Y?" Being bombarded with questions about every single anime is a pain. And while a number is not going to provide any of the reasoning that led to your conclusion, it does at least give an indication of what your tastes and preferences are like. It encourages discussion rather than confusion ("why did you feel this way?", as opposed to "how did you feel about it?) You can't write an essay for every anime in a list environment so numbers suffice.

It's also an easy way to sort your list when it reaches a certain size. You can sort by highest and lowest scores in order to find the shows you liked or enjoyed least. If you have no scores, you have to look at each entry one-by-one and rely on memory. Recommendations become virtually impossible.

I'm all for eliminating review scores, though. They have no reason to exist. The argumentation is already there unlike with your MAL list. Professional websites pretty much do it for the traffic since the majority of people are dumb and can't read more than a Twitter-sized offering.

Excelsior | 04-17-14, 1:21 PM
@RMNDolphy

Ratings are pretty damn deeply problematic and annoying. They're inherently reductionist and hierarchical and flawed. It's true that complex and mutli-faceted thoughts can't be summarized by just plopping down a number of "x/10".

It's also problematic that these numbers mean different things to different users and each person has a different standard for rating. When a person rates something 10/10, what does that mean? Is historical influence being factored in? Or """""objective"""" qualities? Or is it only representative or pure enjoyment? And if a user places, say, Detroit Metal City and Clannad and LOGH all at 10/10, that's even more confusing. Putting all three in the same numerical placement is meaningless because all of those three series have vastly different goals and purposes and, most likely, that user would have vastly different emotional reactions to each work. If this hypothetical user were to cry at Clannad and laugh heartily at DMC and justify it by saying that both deeply moved him, how can one just attach a number to that emotional depth (going back to that whole reductionist shtick)? And does that mean that that user has different rating standards for even different works of different genres (as tenuous as genres already are!)?

And it seems that there's so many internal inconsistencies in rating systems when one looks more microscopically. All series have separate more nuanced goals, don't they? A-and how can something like bad animation be forgiven in something like LOGH but not forgiven in something like Aku no Hana? Tangential to that is just how users can hierarchically rank series into things like "Favorite anime in order from 1-10". The fact that each rating system has different standards for various arbitrary categorizations and classifications and that users attach these different complex thoughts to numbers which can't be summarized without nuanced writing does make these ratings seem almost meaningless.

And, personally, it's hard to justify ratings when things like tags and notes and reviews exist. Even tagging a work with a simplistic "The story is bad" is more meaningful than "4/10".

RMNDolphy | 04-17-14, 11:16 AM
I don't have the patience to nominate this and I'd lose interest by the time it goes up anyway. Why do people rate things?

It seems dumb to me to reduce complex, multifaceted thoughts and feelings towards a work into a single number. To deem a bunch of disparate works (each with their own distinct strengths, weaknesses, approaches, etc) something like "8/10" is beyond oversimplification and seems lazy to me. Words are a much more powerful tool than numbers.

I also think scoring categories individually like on MAL reviews is idiotic. For story, animation, etc to always be given the same weight is to ignore how appropriate/important each is for their respective anime, and the varied ways they work with and against each other. The importance of each varies wildly from one work to the next.

I've also noticed a sort of unhealthy way of watching anime fostered by people preoccupied with scores. People spend an episode reflecting on how minute things change the way they'll score it rather than allowing themselves to be swept up in it. The idea of that seems anti-art to me. Peeps worked hard to make these worlds/stories for an audience to become immersed in; it's disrespectful and in some cases inhibitive to think of the whole thing in terms of arbitrary numerical values. You're ignoring a fundamental part of the work when you do this.

The only tangible benefit I see to it is giving someone a passive sneak peek into your tastes, but the cons outweigh the pros, I think. It goes beyond simply be imprecise. So I'm curious why people do it.

So hey, you. Why do you do it?
