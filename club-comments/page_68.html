twinbells | 01-13-14, 1:35 PM
>But then, that guy from Silver Link was a "student" of Shinbo and... uh...

OONUMA SHIN. Aka. my favourite, favourite bad director (other than Yamakan, but that's more due to what Yammie-kun does outside of directing anime).

>Zetsubou face in Nisekoi

Believe it or not, I think Shinbo—or whoever decided to put that in, because as Kassy said he's doing a lot of sensei-ing these days—is referencing himself. He did it in Katte ni Kaizou (somewhat recommended for SZS fans, absolutely not recommended for anyone else) too, but that makes more sense because the source material is from the same mangaka as SZS.

>Being "generic"

Eh, it's okay. I'm far more interested in execution than "originality"—I don't care how many "generic" romcoms have made if someone (finally) makes a really good one. Just look at American television where like 80% of shows are just cute cops doing cute things, but (from what I'm told by people who actually watch them) these shows vary wildly in quality.

But really, "generic", "mnstrm", "unoriginal"—is there such a thing as a more generic uninspired complaint? None of these qualities is individually enough to sink a show, but I guess it's a lot easier to just give a one word complaint than really break down why something isn't working (in the case of bad romcoms, this shouldn't take any effort at all in most cases). And if in any given anime this highly-valued act of "being different" just entails being more crassly American (ie. not in a good Space Jam sort of way), I'll pass.

Recently, I've seen a lot of people saying stuff like "I expected Pupa to be unique but it was just generic." Although it is certainly bad, Pupa isn't exactly what I would call generic. I suspect that this is just a way to lend a bit of free rhetorical weight to the simple and silly-sounding statement that you expected Pupa to be good, but it was bad. But why are people who are so otherwise willing to make completely empty statements unwilling to say just that? At least it's closer to the truth.

>Being "special"

Now this is interesting and possibly worth making a topic out of. My first thought is that Sekai Seifuku is probably the only entry from this season that counts—aside from glorious white letterboxing the anime, of course—but why is that the case and why doesn't something like Saki count? Magical girls playing mahjong; that's gotta be more special than regular girls playing mahjong. Oh right, it's moeshit—can't be special if its moeshit. More seriously, it's the bog standardy-est bog standard sports anime (g-gross!) underneath everything that makes it fun; I mean, the title character always wins and the plot is just cycling iterations of the fucking Cell Games. But is it fair to just disregard "everything that makes it fun"? I feel like we're conditioned to associate "specialness" with the lack of familiar elements rather than the presence of interesting ones.
